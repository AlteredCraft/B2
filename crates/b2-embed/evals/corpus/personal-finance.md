Money management is a plumbing problem before it is an investing problem:
income flows in, fixed costs drain out, and the design question is where the
remainder goes by default. Systems beat willpower — automatic transfers on
payday route cash to its jobs before there is a chance to spend it — and the
order of operations matters far more than picking clever investments.

## Budgeting

The 50/30/20 sketch divides take-home pay into needs, wants, and saving, and
its real value is the conversation it forces about which is which. A budget
that requires logging every latte dies within a month; a budget that caps
categories and auto-sweeps the rest survives. Review the fixed costs annually —
insurance, subscriptions, phone plans — because a single renegotiated bill
outweighs a year of small denials.

Track net worth quarterly rather than daily. The day-to-day number is noise;
the direction over quarters is the signal that the plumbing works.

## The cushion

Before any investing, park three to six months of bare-bones expenses somewhere
boring and instantly reachable — a high-yield savings account, not the market.
This emergency fund is insurance, not an investment: its job is to turn a blown
head gasket, a burst pipe, or a sudden layoff from a debt spiral into an
inconvenience. Size it to obligations, not income — a renter with no
dependents needs less runway than a homeowner with two kids — and refill it
first after any withdrawal.

Keep it in a separate institution from the checking account. Friction in the
wrong direction is a feature: two days of transfer delay stops the fund from
quietly becoming a vacation account.

## Retirement accounts

Tax-advantaged space is use-it-or-lose-it: each year's contribution room
expires with the year. The employer match in a workplace plan is an instant
hundred-percent return and comes before every other goal; beyond the match,
the choice between pre-tax and after-tax contributions is a bet on whether
today's tax rate or retirement's will be higher. The Roth variant taxes the
money going in and then never again — growth and qualified withdrawals both
come out untaxed — which makes it the default recommendation early in a career,
while earnings and tax brackets are still low.

## Accounts at a glance

| Account | Tax treatment | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Checking | none | the monthly flow, two weeks of buffer |
| High-yield savings | interest taxed as income | the emergency cushion |
| Workplace 401(k) | pre-tax in, taxed out | the match, then bulk saving |
| Roth IRA | taxed in, untaxed growth and withdrawals | early-career decades of compounding |
| Brokerage | capital gains rates | goals before age 59½, overflow |

## Index funds

Once the cushion exists and the tax-advantaged space is filling, the boring
answer is a broad, low-cost index fund: own the whole market, pay a few
hundredths of a percent in fees, and let decades of compounding do the work.
The expense ratio is the one number that predicts fund performance reliably —
every basis point of fee is a basis point of return surrendered every single
year. Rebalance on a calendar, ignore forecasts, and treat a market drop as a
sale on future returns rather than a reason to sell.
