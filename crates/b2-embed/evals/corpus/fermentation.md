Controlled spoilage, essentially: give the right microbes a head start and they
transform sugar and starch into acid, gas, and alcohol before the wrong microbes
can rot the food. Almost every pantry staple with depth — bread, cheese, soy
sauce, pickles, beer — passed through a jar or a vat where bacteria and yeast
did the actual cooking. The craft is mostly about setting conditions (salt,
temperature, time, oxygen) and then leaving things alone.

## Vegetables

Shredded cabbage, daikon, carrots, or green beans go under a salt brine —
roughly two percent salt by weight — which selects for lactic-acid bacteria
while holding off everything else. The one non-negotiable rule is submersion:
any shred poking above the liquid line will host surface molds. Weigh the
vegetables down with a glass disc or a zip bag of brine, and burp the jar daily
during the vigorous first week while carbon dioxide pushes the level up.

Cabbage prepared this way sours slowly at cellar temperatures into something
crisp and tangy; the napa-and-chili version from Korea runs saltier, spicier,
and colder, and many households age it in the refrigerator for months, where it
keeps developing. When the bubbling subsides and the sourness tastes right,
move the jar to cold storage to slow the culture to a crawl.

## Sourdough

A sourdough starter is a domesticated colony: equal weights of flour and water,
left out, catch wild yeast and lactic bacteria from the grain and the air.
Feeding it — discarding half and stirring in fresh flour and water on a
schedule — keeps the colony young and vigorous; a culture fed daily at room
temperature doubles within hours and smells of yogurt and green apple. Neglect
turns it sluggish and sharply vinegary, though a few days of regular meals
usually revives even a gray, separated jar.

Bread made from it rises on the gas the colony exhales, and the long
fermentation both flavors the crumb and partially breaks down the flour,
which is why the loaves keep longer than commercially yeasted ones.

## Drinks

Sweetened tea inoculated with a rubbery culture disc ferments into a tart,
lightly fizzy drink over one to two weeks. The disc — a cellulose mat that
houses the yeast-and-bacteria partnership — floats or sinks indifferently, and
a new layer forms on the surface with each batch. For carbonation, bottle the
finished tea with a spoon of sugar and cap it tightly for a few days at room
temperature, then chill; the trapped gas dissolves and the pour comes up
sparkling.

Water kefir and ginger bug sodas work on the same principle with grain-like
crystals or a homemade ginger culture, on faster cycles of two to three days.

## Temperature and time

| Ferment | Holding range | Typical time |
|---|---|---|
| Sauerkraut | 18–22 °C | 4–6 weeks |
| Kimchi | 12–16 °C | 2–3 weeks |
| Sourdough starter | 24–27 °C | 5–7 days to establish |
| Kombucha | 24–29 °C | 7–14 days |
| Yogurt | 43–46 °C | 6–12 hours |

Warmer runs ferment faster but coarser — acids race ahead of the slower flavor
compounds — while a cooler, longer schedule builds complexity. Below the range
a culture stalls; well above it, the microbes you wanted die and the ones you
didn't take over.

## Troubleshooting

A white, wrinkled film on a vegetable brine is usually kahm yeast: harmless,
skimmed off, a sign the temperature ran warm or the salt ran low. Fuzzy spots
in blue, green, or black are mold — that batch is done, no scraping rescues
it. Slimy or ropy brine early in a ferment often tightens back up as acidity
climbs, so wait a week before judging.

If nothing is bubbling at all after three days, the culture never got going:
the brine was too salty, the room too cold, or chlorinated tap water knocked
the bacteria down. Restart with filtered water and a fresh two-percent brine
rather than nursing a dead jar.
