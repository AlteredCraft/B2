Multi-day walking is a logistics problem wearing a scenery costume: everything
you might need for sleeping, eating, weather, and repair has to ride on your
back, so every object earns its place or stays home. Gear lists converge on the
same skeleton — shelter, sleep, carry, kitchen, water, feet — and diverge only
in how much weight each hiker tolerates for comfort.

## The big three

Shelter, sleep system, and the pack itself dominate the scale, which is why
upgrades start here. A double-wall tent is forgiving and warm; a single-wall
shelter or a tarp sheds hundreds of grams at the cost of condensation
management and pitching skill. The sleep system pairs an insulated pad with a
quilt or mummy bag rated a few degrees below the coldest night you expect —
the pad's insulation matters as much as the bag's, since compressed down under
your back insulates almost nothing.

The pack comes last: buy it after the rest, sized to what remains. A frameless
sack carries a small, light load beautifully and punishes an overstuffed one;
a framed pack hauls bear canisters and week-long food without biting your
shoulders.

## Kitchen and water

A canister stove boils half a liter in about three minutes and packs inside the
pot; alcohol and solid-fuel setups are lighter still but slower and banned
during fire season in many ranges. Cold-soaking skips the stove entirely — 
couscous, ramen, and refried beans rehydrate in a jar while you walk.

For water, a hollow-fiber squeeze filter handles bacteria and protozoa and
weighs under a hundred grams; chemical drops back it up when the filter clogs
or the temperature drops below freezing, which ruptures the fibers. Camel up at
reliable sources and carry capacity for the dry stretches rather than hauling
liters everywhere.

## Footwear

Trail runners have largely displaced leather boots outside of snow and heavy
scrambling: they dry overnight, weigh a third as much, and need no break-in.
The classic rule of thumb says a kilogram on the feet costs as much energy as
five on the back. Waterproof liners cut both ways — they keep splash out for an
hour and then keep sweat and creek water in all day — so many hikers accept wet
feet and carry dry sleep socks instead.

Feet fail small before they fail big. The moment a heel or toe registers a hot
spot, stop and cover it: a patch of moleskin or a strip of tape over the
rubbing skin, applied early, is the difference between a forgettable afternoon
and a week of limping. Trim toenails before the trip, lace lockdown at the
ankle for descents, and air feet out at lunch. Once a blister has fully formed,
drain it with a sterilized needle at the edge, keep the roof of skin on, and
pad around it rather than on top.

## Base weight

Base weight is the pack minus food, water, and fuel — the number that stays
constant trip to trip.

| Style | Base weight | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional | 14–20 kg | maximum comfort in camp, slowest miles |
| Lightweight | under 9 kg | mainstream gear, few skills required |
| Ultralight | under 4.5 kg | every item multi-use, weather skill assumed |

Cutting from traditional to lightweight is mostly shopping; cutting to
ultralight is mostly judgment, because the margin for a wrong forecast or a
lost glove shrinks with the pack.

## Packing

Heavy and dense rides against the spine between the shoulder blades; the sleep
system fills the bottom as crush material; the shelter goes where it can deploy
first in rain. Anything needed while moving — snacks, map, sunscreen, rain
shell — lives in outside pockets so the pack never opens on the trail. A pack
liner (a trash compactor bag) waterproofs better than any rain cover, and the
whole kit should reassemble identically every morning so nothing is ever
searched for twice.
